Big Idea

Representation Helps Us Justify Trends

Data management is about collecting information, organising it, displaying it clearly, and then interpreting it so that conclusions are justified.

What We Need to Be Able to Do

Community Context

What Can Data Show?

Characteristics

What is true about a group at one time?

Example: Most students travel to school by bus.

Trends

How does something change over time or between groups?

Example: Cycling to school increased from Year 1 to Year 5.

What issue in your school or community could be investigated with data?

Data Types

Qualitative and Quantitative

Qualitative Data

Words, labels, descriptions, or ordered categories.

  • Categorical
  • Ordinal

Quantitative Data

Numerical data that describes quantities.

  • Discrete
  • Continuous

Four Categories

Classifying Data

Categorical

Groups with no natural order.

Music genre, transport type, hair colour.

Ordinal

Categories with an order.

Rating 1-5, low-medium-high, MYP year.

Discrete

Countable numbers.

Number of siblings, pets, points scored.

Continuous

Measured numbers that can take values between values.

Height, time, distance, mass.

Collecting Data

Questions Must Match the Data You Need

Less useful

Do you use social media too much?

This is biased and does not define what "too much" means.

More useful

How many minutes did you spend on social media yesterday?

This produces measurable data that can be represented.

Before collecting data, decide what characteristic or trend you are trying to uncover.

Frequency Tables

When Are They Useful?

Use when

  • You are counting categories or repeated values.
  • You need to check the total number of responses.
  • You want a table before drawing a graph.

Be careful when

  • There are too many different values.
  • Continuous data needs intervals.
  • The categories are unclear or overlap.

Good Graphs

Requirements for Clear Representation

  • Use a clear and relevant title.
  • Label axes and include units where needed.
  • Use an appropriate and evenly spaced scale.
  • Use a legend or key when there is more than one group.
  • Make the graph large enough to read.
  • Choose a graph type that fits the data and the question.

Bar Graph

What It Shows

Use for

  • Categorical data
  • Ordinal data
  • Discrete data with clear values

The bars should have equal width. The height shows the frequency or percentage.

Bar Graph

How to Draw It by Hand

  1. Make a frequency table or list the categories and values.
  2. Draw two axes. Put categories on the horizontal axis and frequency or percentage on the vertical axis.
  3. Choose an even vertical scale that fits the largest value.
  4. Label both axes and include units if needed.
  5. Draw bars with equal widths and equal gaps.
  6. Add a title. If there are multiple groups, add a colour-coded legend.
For a bar graph, the bar heights matter. The bar widths should not carry meaning.

Histogram

Grouped Continuous Data

How it differs from a bar graph

  • The horizontal axis is made of intervals.
  • The bars touch because the intervals are continuous.
  • It shows the shape of a numerical distribution.

Histogram

How to Draw It by Hand

  1. Choose equal intervals for the continuous data, such as 0-15, 16-30, 31-45 minutes.
  2. Count how many data values fit in each interval.
  3. Draw axes and place intervals on the horizontal axis.
  4. Put frequency on the vertical axis and choose an even scale.
  5. Draw bars that touch, because the intervals continue from one to the next.
  6. Add a clear title and labels with units.
Do not use a histogram for categories like sport, colour, or music genre. Those need a bar graph.

Line Graph

Showing Change and Trends

Use for

  • Continuous data over time
  • Time series
  • Prediction from visible patterns

A line graph is created by joining data points with lines.

Line Graph

How to Draw It by Hand

  1. Put time on the horizontal axis if the data is a time series.
  2. Put the measured variable on the vertical axis.
  3. Label both axes and include units.
  4. Choose scales that are evenly distributed. The horizontal and vertical scales may be different.
  5. Plot each ordered pair carefully.
  6. Join neighbouring points with straight line segments.
  7. Add a title and a legend if there is more than one line.

Circle Graph

Parts of a Whole

Use for

  • Categorical parts of one total
  • Percentages that add to 100%
  • Showing how a whole is divided

It is usually weak for showing trends over time.

Circle Graph

How to Calculate Sector Angles

A full circle has 360 degrees. Each sector must be proportional to the amount of data it represents.

sector angle = category fraction x 360

Circle Graph

How to Draw It by Hand

  1. Find the total number of data values.
  2. Calculate each category as a fraction or percentage of the total.
  3. Multiply each fraction by 360 to find the sector angle.
  4. Check that all sector angles add to 360 degrees.
  5. Draw a circle with a compass and mark the centre.
  6. Draw a radius to use as the starting line.
  7. Use a protractor to measure each sector from the last line drawn.
  8. Add a title, labels or percentages, and a colour-coded key.

Multiple circle graphs

When comparing graphs, use the same scale whenever possible. Otherwise the comparison may be misleading.

Interpretation

Characteristics vs Trends

Characteristic

A feature visible in the data.

Example: Car travel is more common for high school students than middle school students.

Trend

A pattern of change.

Example: As students get older, car travel appears to increase and public transport appears to decrease.

Scale Can Change the Story

Misleading Graphs

What to Watch For

  • A vertical scale that cuts off important context.
  • Different scales used for graphs that are being compared.
  • Titles or headlines that claim more than the data shows.
  • Missing units, missing labels, or unclear categories.
  • A graph type that hides the key relationship.
  • A table or graph that does not show whether the data is a sample or a whole population.

Graph Checklist

Before You Say a Graph Is Finished

Construction

  • Title is specific.
  • Axes are labelled.
  • Scale is even and appropriate.
  • Bars, sectors, or points match the data.
  • Legend is clear when needed.

Interpretation

  • Conclusion uses numbers from the graph.
  • Trend or characteristic is named clearly.
  • Limits of the data are acknowledged.
  • Predictions are justified by the pattern.

Interpretation Frame

How to Explain Results

The data shows that __________.

The strongest evidence is __________.

This suggests the characteristic/trend __________.

However, the graph does not show __________.

To investigate further, I would collect __________.

A strong conclusion uses actual values from the graph and avoids claiming causes unless the data supports them.

Mini Project

Community Profile

Choose a question about your class, school, neighbourhood, or community.

  1. Collect data by survey, observation, or a reliable source.
  2. Classify each variable as categorical, ordinal, discrete, or continuous.
  3. Make a table to organise the data.
  4. Create at least two different graphs.
  5. Decide which graph represents the data most effectively and justify your choice.

Project Requirements

What the Final Work Must Include

  • A clear data question and source of data.
  • At least one table.
  • A bar graph or multiple bar graph.
  • A circle graph or multiple circle graphs.
  • A line graph or multiple line graph if there is change over time.
  • Titles, labelled axes, clear legends, and appropriate scale.
  • A paragraph explaining characteristics, trends, predictions, and limitations.

Key Takeaway

Choose the Graph That Makes the Relationship Clear

The same data can often be represented in more than one way, but the best representation depends on the type of data and the conclusion you need to justify.
