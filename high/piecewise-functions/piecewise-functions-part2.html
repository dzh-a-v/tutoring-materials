Theory · Method

How to solve piecewise problems

A reliable five-step recipe that works on every problem in this deck

  • 1
    Read the function as a table. Each row is one piece — a formula f(x) together with an interval that says when the formula applies (its domain).
  • 2
    Locate x in an interval. For the input you are given, ask: which piece does it belong to? At a boundary, check whether the inequality is strict (<, >) or inclusive (≤, ≥).
  • 3
    Pick exactly one piece. Substitute the value into the matching formula. Never average two pieces and never use both at once.
  • 4
    Compute and label. Evaluate carefully. Write the result with units (dollars, hours, °C…) so the meaning is clear.
  • 5
    Sanity-check at the joins. Plug a boundary value into both pieces. If the values agree, the function is continuous; if not, mark the jump.

Pitfalls to avoid

  • using the wrong piece because you skimmed the inequality
  • forgetting that ≤ includes the endpoint, < does not
  • plugging into both pieces and adding the answers
  • dropping units or signs in the second piece
Mantra · "One x, one piece, one answer."
Going backwards · if the output is given (e.g. "find x so that f(x) = 20"), find which piece's range contains 20, then solve that piece's equation for x.

Theory · Purpose

Why we build piecewise functions

Real life rarely follows one rule from start to finish

A piecewise function is a single function whose rule changes when the input crosses certain boundaries. We use them whenever a real-world quantity follows different rules in different ranges, but we still want one formula that captures the whole behaviour.

💸 Money & pricing

  • Tax brackets, electricity tariffs, shipping costs
  • "First N units cheap, then more expensive"
  • Phone & data plans (Problem 1) and overtime pay

🚗 Motion & physics

  • An object that accelerates, then cruises, then stops
  • Heating water from ice → liquid → steam
  • Toll fees that change at a fixed distance

📋 Rules & thresholds

  • Grade boundaries: 0–49 fail, 50–69 pass, 70+ merit
  • Working hours: regular pay vs overtime
  • Speed limits that change between zones

The big idea

A piecewise function takes messy reality — one rule here, a different rule there — and packages it into a single mathematical object we can evaluate, graph, optimise and reason about. That is why engineers, economists, scientists and game programmers all use them daily.

Question to keep in mind · for any quantity you study, ask "does the rule ever change?" The moment the answer is yes — at a threshold, a deadline, a boundary — you have a piecewise function on your hands.

Guide · Part 1

Choosing the dimensions of your graph

Six decisions to make before the pencil touches paper

  • 1
    Identify the variables. What is on the horizontal axis (input x) and on the vertical axis (output f(x))? Always include the units.
  • 2
    Find the domain you need. Look at every piece. The domain is the union of all sub-intervals — for example x ≥ 0, or 0 ≤ x ≤ 12.
  • 3
    Find the range. Evaluate every piece at its endpoints (and any vertex of a parabola). The smallest output gives the lower bound, the largest gives the upper bound.
  • 4
    Add a comfort margin. Extend each axis ~10% past the data so the graph doesn't feel cramped against the edge.
  • 5
    Pick a sensible scale. Choose tick spacing in round numbers (1, 2, 5, 10, 20, 50, 100…). The two axes can use different scales — match the data.
  • 6
    Mark the joins. Plot a filled dot ● where the piece includes the endpoint, and an open dot ○ where it excludes it.

Guide · Part 2

Drawing the graph, piece by piece

A worked walk-through using a small two-piece function

Our function

f(x) = {
x + 2 if  x < 1
−x + 4 if  x ≥ 1
  • 1
    Draw the axes. Mark equal ticks on both. Label them with the variable names.
  • 2
    Plot piece 1. For x < 1, plot two points such as (−2, 0) and (0, 2). Continue the line up to x = 1 and end with an open dot at (1, 3).
  • 3
    Plot piece 2. For x ≥ 1, plot (1, 3) with a filled dot, then (4, 0). Draw the segment between them.
  • 4
    Read the join. Both pieces meet at (1, 3) — the function is continuous. If they hadn't, you would mark the gap with both an open and a filled dot.

Piece 1 (open at join) Piece 2 (filled at join)
Open vs filled dots · ≤ or ≥ ⇒ filled, < or > ⇒ open. At a continuity point one piece supplies the filled dot and the open from the other piece sits underneath — visually they overlap, which is fine.

Problem 5

Parking garage rates

A flat starting fee, then a per-hour charge with a daily cap

A parking garage in town charges drivers as follows:

  • $4 flat fee for parking up to and including 1 hour
  • $2 per additional hour after the first hour
  • A maximum charge of $20 once you have parked 9 hours or more (up to 24 h)

Cost function

C(t) = {
4 if  0 ≤ t ≤ 1
4 + 2(t − 1) if  1 < t < 9
20 if  9 ≤ t ≤ 24

where t is the number of hours parked.

FIND

  • (a) Cost for 0.5 hour
  • (b) Cost for 1 hour
  • (c) Cost for 4 hours
  • (d) Cost for 12 hours
Notice · The numbers are chosen so the function is continuous: piece 2 evaluated at t = 9 gives 4 + 2(8) = 20, which agrees with piece 3.

Solution 5

Parking garage rates

Pick the matching piece, then evaluate

(a) t = 0.5 · since 0 ≤ 0.5 ≤ 1, use the first piece

C(0.5) = $4

(b) t = 1 · boundary, top piece (≤)

C(1) = $4

(c) t = 4 · since 1 < 4 < 9, use the middle piece

C(4) = 4 + 2(4 − 1) = 4 + 6 = $10

(d) t = 12 · since 9 ≤ 12 ≤ 24, the daily cap applies

C(12) = $20
Parking garage cost C(t)
Piece 1: flat $4 Piece 2: per-hour Piece 3: $20 cap
The cost grows linearly between hours 1 and 9, then locks at the daily cap. The function is continuous everywhere — no jumps.

Problem 6

Weekly pay with overtime

A standard hourly rate, then time-and-a-half after 40 hours

A worker is paid:

  • $15 per hour for the first 40 hours of the week
  • $22.50 per hour ("time-and-a-half") for any hour beyond 40
  • The week is capped at 60 hours

Pay function

P(h) = {
15h if  0 ≤ h ≤ 40
600 + 22.5(h − 40) if  40 < h ≤ 60

where h is the number of hours worked in the week.

FIND

  • (a) Pay for 25 hours
  • (b) Pay for 40 hours
  • (c) Pay for 50 hours
  • (d) How many hours produce a pay of $825?
Going backwards · part (d) is a reverse question. First decide which piece has output 825, then solve that piece's equation.

Solution 6

Weekly pay with overtime

Forward (h → P) and reverse (P → h) — both directions matter

(a) h = 25 · since 0 ≤ 25 ≤ 40, top piece

P(25) = 15 × 25 = $375

(b) h = 40 · boundary, top piece (≤)

P(40) = 15 × 40 = $600

(c) h = 50 · since 40 < 50 ≤ 60, bottom piece

P(50) = 600 + 22.5(50 − 40) = 600 + 225 = $825

(d) Pay $825 > $600, so we are in the bottom piece. Solve:

825 = 600 + 22.5(h − 40)
225 = 22.5(h − 40)
h − 40 = 10  ⇒  h = 50 hours

Consistent with part (c) — a useful self-check.

Weekly pay P(h)
Regular: $15/h Overtime: $22.50/h
The graph is one connected curve — the function is continuous. Only the slope changes at h = 40, and the steeper slope is the overtime rate.

Problem 7

Find the constant — continuity

A classic exam favourite: choose k so the pieces fit perfectly

Consider the function

f(x) = {
2x + 1 if  x ≤ 2
x² + k if  x > 2
  • The first piece is a straight line
  • The second piece is a parabola, shifted up by k
  • For the function to make sense as a single graph, the two pieces must meet at x = 2

FIND

  • (a) The value of k that makes f continuous at x = 2
  • (b) f(2) with that k
  • (c) f(5) with that k
Continuity test · the value the left piece gives at the boundary must equal the value the right piece gives there.

Solution 7

Find the constant — continuity

Set the two pieces equal at the join, then solve for k

(a) Force the pieces to agree at x = 2

left piece at 2:  2(2) + 1 = 5
right piece at 2:  (2)² + k = 4 + k
set equal:  4 + k = 5  ⇒  k = 1
k = 1

(b) f(2) with k = 1

Use the top piece (it includes x = 2):

f(2) = 2(2) + 1 = 5

(c) f(5) with k = 1

Use the bottom piece (5 > 2):

f(5) = 5² + 1 = 25 + 1 = 26
Continuous piecewise function (k = 1)
2x + 1, x ≤ 2 x² + 1, x > 2
With k = 1 the pieces meet exactly at (2, 5) — no jump. For any other value of k the parabola would land above or below 5 and the graph would tear.

Wrap-up

What you can now do

Three big skills, ready to take into any new piecewise problem

1 · Solve

  • Locate x in the right interval
  • Substitute into one piece only
  • Verify continuity at every join
  • Reverse-solve when the output is given

2 · Build

  • Translate "rule changes here" into intervals
  • Pick consistent variables and units
  • Make sure every input has exactly one output
  • Decide if the function should be continuous

3 · Graph

  • Choose axes, range, scale and ticks deliberately
  • Use filled ● and open ○ dots correctly
  • Plot lines from endpoints; curves from samples
  • Annotate joins, kinks and jumps clearly

One sentence to remember

"A piecewise function is one function with several rules — pick the rule by checking the interval first."
